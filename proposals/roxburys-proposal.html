01 What we heard

The real problem isn't
volume. It's the noise.

Roxbury's isn't short of customers, product, or reputation. What keeps breaking is the ratio of signal to noise across the auction cycle, and the fact that every member of a lean team is paying for it.

Three things came up in the conversation that shape this entire proposal. You said them plainly, and they are the things we're building around.

"Two weeks of hell after every main auction."
Post-auction comms concentrate the pain. 500 invoices, 3,000 lots, payments, tracking, complaints, refunds, fraud, and five regulars who only deal with you personally. The cycle gets through, but it costs the team everything for a fortnight at a time. This is the cycle we're targeting.
"75% of inquiries are rubbish, and the staff are buried in it."
Three quarters of what hits Zendesk and Gmail every week is noise: routine acknowledgements, tracking queries, FAQ, and off-target valuation inquiries. The team spends real hours on work that shouldn't need a human to touch it. That 75% is what the triage layer takes off them.
"We don't need any one person. We need a little bit everywhere."
You named the shape of the solution yourself. Not another hire. A layer of capability distributed across the cycle, coordinated through Slack, handling the volume so the team can focus on customers. That's exactly what an agent unit delivers.
"Melbourne and Sydney will find a gear."
The expansion is the forcing function. What's hectic in Brisbane will be structurally impossible across three offices without something doing the routing, the processing, and the pattern-watching. The unit has to be ready before the volume arrives. This is why we build now, not after.

03 The unit

Five agents,
one accountable lead.

The auction comms unit is designed as one complete operating structure. Scott sits at the centre. The team interacts through Slack. Five named agents handle the layers of work that don't need a human to touch them, and escalate everything that does.

We designed this as a complete unit because that's how it produces compounding value. One agent solves one bottleneck. A unit changes how the whole cycle runs.

Every agent has a specific, scoped remit. Together they cover the auction cycle end to end: intake, pre-auction sign-off, post-auction processing, shipping, and exception watching. Scott stays accountable. The team gets the lift everywhere.

We only fully build Roxy in this first wave. She earns her keep alone, and she builds the foundation the rest of the team deploys on.

A note on scope

This unit is built for one job: carrying the auction comms cycle across Brisbane, Melbourne, and Sydney. It's foundational for the growth of the auction business, not reusable across other parts of Roxbury's.

The valuation side, PCGS Australia, Cotton & Co, and the Australian Coin Review each have their own work units waiting to be built. Each would be a separate team, trained on a different domain, scoped as its own engagement. We flag them in the scope notes so the picture is complete, but nothing about this proposal commits to them.

08 Scope notes

What sits outside
this first build.

A clean first-build scope matters more than a broad one. These items came up in conversation and are worth flagging as separate future units, each scoped to its own part of the business.

Explicitly out of the first engagement
Each of these would be its own unit, with its own agents, trained on its own domain. We keep them out of the first engagement so the auction comms unit gets the focus it needs to land cleanly.
  • Valuation intake unit. The 75% rubbish filter on the deal-flow side. A separate unit, different shape, different domain. Natural as Roxbury's next engagement once the auction comms unit is stable.
  • PCGS Australia status unit. Handles the four-to-six-month grading cycle and the constant "where are my coins" queries. Similar shape, different domain. Separate build.
  • DocuSign vendor signature redesign. A discrete workflow optimisation for the seventy-plus vendors who struggle with digital signatures. Scoped separately if you want it tackled.
  • CRM or project management review. The Asana question. Left out entirely in this scope. If it needs to change, it changes on its own track.
  • Cotton & Co and Australian Coin Review. Each brand has its own comms and customer patterns. Both have unit-shaped problems worth mapping when the auction side is running smoothly.
